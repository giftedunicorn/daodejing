# Dao De Jing Natural English Translation

This is a natural English rendering of the [Dao De Jing source text](./daodejing.md), aligned chapter by chapter with the Chinese natural translation in [daodejing-translation.md](./daodejing-translation.md). It is written for readability and reflection, not as a strict philological edition.

## Chapter 1

The Dao that can be fully spoken is not the constant Dao. The name that can be fixed in words is not the constant name. Without name, it is the beginning of heaven and earth; with name, it is the mother of all things. Free from desire, one may observe its subtle mystery; with desire, one sees only its outer boundary. These two arise from the same source, though they are called by different names. Together they are mystery. Mystery within mystery: the gate of all wonders.

## Chapter 2

When everyone knows beauty as beauty, ugliness has already appeared. When everyone knows goodness as goodness, what is not good has already appeared. Being and nonbeing generate each other; difficult and easy complete each other; long and short measure each other; high and low depend on each other; sound and voice harmonize with each other; before and after follow each other. Therefore the sage acts through non-forcing and teaches without excessive words. The ten thousand things arise, and he does not reject them. He gives life but does not possess, acts but does not claim, completes the work but does not dwell on it. Because he does not cling to it, it does not leave him.

## Chapter 3

Do not overpraise the talented, and people will not compete for status. Do not prize rare goods, and people will not become thieves. Do not display what inflames desire, and people's hearts will not be disturbed. Thus the sage governs by emptying restless minds, filling bellies, weakening ambition, and strengthening bones. He keeps people from being driven by cleverness and craving, and keeps those who think themselves clever from reckless action. Govern by non-forcing, and nothing will be left ungoverned.

## Chapter 4

The Dao is empty, yet its use never runs dry. It is deep, as if it were the ancestor of all things. It blunts sharpness, untangles knots, softens brightness, and joins with the dust. It is clear and still, as if it has always been. I do not know whose child it is. It seems to have existed before the lord of heaven.

## Chapter 5

Heaven and earth are not partial; they treat all things like straw dogs. The sage is not partial; he treats the people in the same way. The space between heaven and earth is like a bellows: empty, yet never exhausted; the more it moves, the more it brings forth. Too many words quickly run dry. Better to hold to the center.

## Chapter 6

The spirit of the valley does not die. It is called the mysterious female. The gate of the mysterious female is the root of heaven and earth. It continues faintly and endlessly. Use it gently, and it is never exhausted.

## Chapter 7

Heaven is long-lasting, and earth endures. They endure because they do not live for themselves; therefore they can last. So the sage places himself behind and comes to the front; he puts himself outside and is preserved. Is this not because he is free of selfishness? Because he is free of selfishness, he is able to fulfill himself.

## Chapter 8

The highest goodness is like water. Water benefits all things and does not contend. It stays in places people dislike, and so it is close to the Dao. In dwelling, value the right place. In heart, value depth. In relationships, value kindness. In speech, value trust. In governing, value order. In work, value ability. In action, value timing. Because there is no contention, there is no blame.

## Chapter 9

To keep filling what is already full is not as good as stopping. To sharpen a blade too much is to make it unable to last. A hall filled with gold and jade cannot be guarded forever. Wealth and rank joined with pride leave disaster behind. When the work is complete, withdraw. This is the way of heaven.

## Chapter 10

Can you hold body and spirit together and keep them from separating? Can you gather the breath and become as soft as an infant? Can you cleanse the inner mirror until it has no stain? Can you love the people and govern the state without clever manipulation? Can you open and close the gate of heaven while remaining receptive? Can you understand all directions without claiming knowledge? To give life and nurture, to create without possessing, to act without relying on the act, to lead without dominating: this is called mysterious virtue.

## Chapter 11

Thirty spokes join at one hub; because of the empty space, the wheel is useful. Clay is shaped into a vessel; because of the empty space, the vessel is useful. Doors and windows are cut to make a room; because of the empty space, the room is useful. What is present brings benefit; what is absent makes use possible.

## Chapter 12

Too many colors blind the eye. Too many sounds deafen the ear. Too many flavors dull the taste. Racing and hunting drive the heart mad. Rare goods make conduct go astray. Therefore the sage cares for the belly, not the eye. He leaves that and takes this.

## Chapter 13

Favor and disgrace both startle. Great trouble is tied to having a self. What does it mean that favor and disgrace both startle? Favor is unstable and lowly: gaining it startles, losing it startles. This is favor and disgrace startling alike. What does it mean that great trouble is tied to having a self? I have great trouble because I have a self to protect. If I were not attached to myself, what trouble would I have? Therefore one who values his body as the world may be trusted with the world; one who loves his body as the world may be entrusted with the world.

## Chapter 14

Look for it and you cannot see it: it is called subtle. Listen for it and you cannot hear it: it is called rarefied. Reach for it and you cannot grasp it: it is called minute. These three cannot be fully examined, so they merge into one. Above, it is not bright; below, it is not dark. It stretches on without name and returns to no-thing. It is the form of the formless, the image of no object, called dim and indistinct. Meet it and you do not see its front; follow it and you do not see its back. Hold to the ancient Dao to guide what exists now. To know the ancient beginning is to know the thread of the Dao.

## Chapter 15

Those in ancient times who were good at the Dao were subtle, mysterious, and deeply penetrating. Because they cannot be fully known, we can only describe their appearance: cautious, as if crossing a winter river; alert, as if fearing neighbors on every side; dignified, like a guest; open, like ice about to melt; plain, like uncarved wood; spacious, like a valley; mixed, like muddy water. Who can let muddy water settle until it slowly clears? Who can remain still until life slowly returns? One who keeps this Dao does not seek fullness. Because he is not full, he can remain covered and does not rush to be newly made.

## Chapter 16

Reach the utmost emptiness. Guard deep stillness. The ten thousand things arise together, and I watch their return. All things flourish, then each returns to its root. Returning to the root is called stillness; this is returning to life. Returning to life is called constancy; knowing constancy is clarity. Not knowing constancy leads to reckless action and danger. Knowing constancy brings capacity. Capacity brings fairness. Fairness brings sovereignty. Sovereignty follows heaven. Heaven follows the Dao. The Dao endures. One who follows it is not endangered throughout life.

## Chapter 17

The best ruler is one the people barely know exists. Next comes one they love and praise. Next, one they fear. Last, one they despise. When trust is insufficient, distrust appears. The best ruler is careful with words. When the work is done and the task is complete, the people all say, "We did it ourselves."

## Chapter 18

When the great Dao is abandoned, benevolence and righteousness appear. When cleverness and knowledge arise, great falseness appears. When family relationships are not in harmony, filial piety and parental kindness are emphasized. When the state is in disorder, loyal ministers appear.

## Chapter 19

Cut off the worship of sagely cleverness, and people will benefit a hundredfold. Cut off attachment to benevolence and righteousness, and people will return to natural family care. Cut off craftiness and profit-seeking, and thieves will disappear. Yet these three sayings are not enough as decoration. Give people something to return to: show plainness, hold simplicity, reduce selfishness, and lessen desire.

## Chapter 20

End the pursuit of excessive learning, and there will be no anxiety. How far apart are yes and yes-sir? How far apart are good and evil? What people fear, one cannot simply refuse to fear. Vast and unfinished! The crowd is delighted, as if enjoying a great feast, as if climbing a tower in spring. I alone am quiet before any sign appears, like an infant who has not yet smiled. Drifting, as if with nowhere to return. Everyone has more than enough, while I alone seem to have lost everything. My heart is like that of a fool, dull and confused. Others are bright; I alone seem dim. Others are sharp; I alone seem muffled. Broad like the sea, wandering as if without end. Everyone has a purpose; I alone seem stubborn and crude. I alone differ from others because I value the mother that nourishes all.

## Chapter 21

The form of great virtue follows only the Dao. As a thing, the Dao is vague and elusive. Elusive and vague, within it there is image. Vague and elusive, within it there is substance. Deep and dark, within it there is essence. That essence is very real; within it there is trust. From ancient times until now, its name has not disappeared. Through it we observe the beginning of all things. How do I know the beginning of all things? By this.

## Chapter 22

To bend is to be preserved. To be crooked is to become straight. To be low is to become full. To be worn is to be renewed. To have little is to receive. To have too much is to be confused. Therefore the sage holds to the One and becomes a model for the world. He does not display himself, so he is clear. He does not insist he is right, so he is recognized. He does not boast, so he has merit. He does not exalt himself, so he lasts. Because he does not contend, no one in the world can contend with him. The old saying "to bend is to be preserved" is not empty speech. Truly, one is preserved and returns whole.

## Chapter 23

Few words are natural. A violent wind does not last all morning; a sudden rain does not last all day. Who makes these things? Heaven and earth. If even heaven and earth cannot sustain violence for long, how much less can human beings? Therefore one who follows the Dao is joined with the Dao. One who follows virtue is joined with virtue. One who follows loss is joined with loss. The Dao gladly receives one joined with the Dao; virtue gladly receives one joined with virtue; loss gladly receives one joined with loss. When trust is insufficient, distrust appears.

## Chapter 24

One who stands on tiptoe cannot stand firm. One who strides too far cannot walk long. One who displays himself is not clear. One who insists he is right is not recognized. One who boasts has no merit. One who exalts himself does not last. From the view of the Dao, these are leftover food and useless growths. Things dislike them. Therefore one who has the Dao does not dwell in them.

## Chapter 25

There is something formed in wholeness, born before heaven and earth. Silent and vast, standing alone and unchanging, moving everywhere without danger, it may be called the mother of the world. I do not know its name. I call it Dao. Forced to name it, I call it great. Great means moving on; moving on means going far; going far means returning. Therefore the Dao is great, heaven is great, earth is great, and the king is also great. Within the realm there are four greats, and the king is one of them. Humanity follows earth. Earth follows heaven. Heaven follows the Dao. The Dao follows what is naturally so.

## Chapter 26

Heaviness is the root of lightness. Stillness is the master of restlessness. Therefore the noble person travels all day without leaving the baggage carts. Though there are splendid sights, he remains calm and detached. How can the ruler of a great state treat the world lightly? Lightness loses the root. Restlessness loses the master.

## Chapter 27

A good traveler leaves no tracks. A good speaker leaves no flaw. A good counter needs no tally sticks. A good closer uses no bolt, yet it cannot be opened. A good binder uses no rope, yet it cannot be untied. Therefore the sage is always good at saving people, so no one is abandoned; always good at saving things, so nothing is discarded. This is called following clarity. The good person is the teacher of the not-good person; the not-good person is the material of the good person. Not honoring the teacher and not caring for the material is great confusion, however clever one may be. This is the essential mystery.

## Chapter 28

Know the masculine, but keep to the feminine, and become the valley of the world. Being the valley of the world, constant virtue does not leave you, and you return to the state of an infant. Know the white, but keep to the black, and become the model of the world. Being the model of the world, constant virtue does not go astray, and you return to the limitless. Know honor, but keep to humility, and become the low valley of the world. Being the low valley of the world, constant virtue is sufficient, and you return to simplicity. When simplicity is divided, it becomes tools. When the sage uses it, he becomes a leader. Great shaping does not cut apart.

## Chapter 29

Those who want to take the world and manipulate it will not succeed. The world is a sacred vessel. It cannot be forced and cannot be seized. Force it and you ruin it. Grasp it and you lose it. Some things move ahead, some follow. Some breathe out, some breathe in. Some are strong, some are weak. Some support, some overturn. Therefore the sage avoids extremes, extravagance, and excess.

## Chapter 30

One who assists a ruler with the Dao does not use military force to dominate the world, for such force returns upon itself. Where armies have camped, thorns and brambles grow. After great wars, there are years of famine. One who is good stops when the purpose is achieved and does not dare to seek power through force. Achieve the result without boasting, without showing off, without pride. Achieve the result because it was unavoidable. Achieve it without becoming strong and aggressive. Things that become overly strong soon grow old. This is not the Dao. What is not the Dao soon ends.

## Chapter 31

Fine weapons are instruments of ill omen. Things dislike them, so one who has the Dao does not rely on them. In peaceful affairs, the noble person values the left; in war, he values the right. Weapons are instruments of ill omen, not tools of the noble person. He uses them only when there is no choice, and calm restraint is best. Victory should not be treated as beautiful. To delight in victory is to delight in killing. One who delights in killing cannot fulfill his purpose in the world. Auspicious affairs honor the left; funerary affairs honor the right. The deputy general stands on the left, the chief general on the right, meaning war should be treated as mourning. When many people have been killed, meet it with grief. After victory, observe it as a funeral.

## Chapter 32

The Dao is always nameless. Though simplicity is small, nothing under heaven can command it. If rulers could keep it, all things would come of themselves. Heaven and earth would join and sweet dew would fall. Without orders, people would naturally become balanced. Once institutions begin, names appear. Once names exist, one must know when to stop. Knowing when to stop prevents danger. The Dao in the world is like rivers and seas to valleys and streams: all return to it.

## Chapter 33

Knowing others is intelligence. Knowing yourself is clarity. Overcoming others is strength. Overcoming yourself is true power. Knowing contentment is wealth. Acting with persistence is resolve. One who does not lose his root lasts long. One who dies yet does not perish has true longevity.

## Chapter 34

The great Dao flows everywhere, to the left and to the right. All things depend on it for life, and it does not refuse them. It completes its work but does not claim ownership. It clothes and nourishes all things but does not rule them. Always without private desire, it may be called small. All things return to it, yet it does not rule them, so it may be called great. Because it never makes itself great, it is able to accomplish greatness.

## Chapter 35

Hold the great image, and the world will come. It will come and not be harmed, finding peace, security, and rest. Music and fine food make travelers stop. But when the Dao is spoken, it seems plain and without flavor. Look at it and there is not enough to see. Listen to it and there is not enough to hear. Use it, and it cannot be exhausted.

## Chapter 36

If you want something to contract, first allow it to expand. If you want to weaken something, first allow it to grow strong. If you want something to fall, first allow it to rise. If you want to take something, first allow it to be given. This is subtle clarity. The soft and weak overcome the hard and strong. Fish should not leave the deep water. The state's sharp instruments should not be shown to people.

## Chapter 37

The Dao is always without forcing, yet nothing is left undone. If rulers could keep to it, all things would transform by themselves. If desire arises during transformation, I would settle it with nameless simplicity. Nameless simplicity brings freedom from desire. Without desire there is stillness, and the world settles itself.

## Chapter 38

The highest virtue does not cling to virtue; therefore it has virtue. Lesser virtue tries not to lose virtue; therefore it lacks true virtue. The highest virtue does not force and has no agenda. Lesser virtue acts and has an agenda. Highest benevolence acts but has no agenda. Highest righteousness acts and has an agenda. Highest ritual acts, and when no one responds, it stretches out its arms to force compliance. Thus when the Dao is lost, virtue appears. When virtue is lost, benevolence appears. When benevolence is lost, righteousness appears. When righteousness is lost, ritual appears. Ritual is the thinning of loyalty and trust, and the beginning of disorder. Forecasting is the flower of the Dao and the beginning of folly. Therefore the mature person dwells in what is thick, not what is thin; in the fruit, not the flower. He leaves that and takes this.

## Chapter 39

Those of old attained the One: heaven attained the One and became clear; earth attained the One and became stable; spirits attained the One and became potent; valleys attained the One and became full; all things attained the One and came to life; rulers attained the One and became standards for the world. Without it, heaven might split, earth might shake, spirits might fade, valleys might dry up, all things might perish, and rulers might fall. Therefore the noble is rooted in the lowly, and the high is based on what is below. For this reason rulers call themselves lonely, lacking, and unworthy. Is this not taking humility as the root? The highest praise does not rely on praise. Do not wish to be polished like jade or rough like stone.

## Chapter 40

Returning is the movement of the Dao. Weakness is the function of the Dao. All things under heaven are born from being, and being is born from nonbeing.

## Chapter 41

When a superior person hears the Dao, he practices it diligently. When an average person hears the Dao, he half keeps it and half loses it. When an inferior person hears the Dao, he laughs loudly. If it were not laughed at, it would not be enough to be the Dao. Therefore it is said: the bright Dao seems dark; the advancing Dao seems to retreat; the level Dao seems uneven. Highest virtue seems like a valley. Great purity seems stained. Vast virtue seems insufficient. Solid virtue seems slack. Simple truth seems changeable. The great square has no corners. The great vessel is completed late. The great sound is barely heard. The great image has no form. The Dao is hidden and nameless, yet it is good at completing all things.

## Chapter 42

The Dao gives birth to One. One gives birth to Two. Two gives birth to Three. Three gives birth to the ten thousand things. The ten thousand things carry yin and embrace yang; through the blending of vital breath they reach harmony. What people dislike are loneliness, lack, and unworthiness, yet rulers use these names for themselves. Thus some things are diminished and thereby increased; some are increased and thereby diminished. What others have taught, I also teach: the violent do not die well. I take this as the root of teaching.

## Chapter 43

The softest thing in the world can move through the hardest thing in the world. What has no form can enter where there is no gap. From this I know the benefit of non-forcing. Teaching without words and the benefit of non-forcing are rarely matched in the world.

## Chapter 44

Name or body: which is closer? Body or wealth: which matters more? Gain or loss: which is more harmful? Excessive attachment brings great cost. Excessive storing brings heavy loss. Knowing contentment prevents disgrace. Knowing when to stop prevents danger. In this way one can last.

## Chapter 45

Great completion seems incomplete, yet its use does not wear out. Great fullness seems empty, yet its use is not exhausted. Great straightness seems bent. Great skill seems clumsy. Great eloquence seems slow. Movement overcomes cold; stillness overcomes heat. Clear stillness becomes the standard of the world.

## Chapter 46

When the world has the Dao, war horses return to the fields. When the world lacks the Dao, pregnant mares give birth near battlefields. No disaster is greater than not knowing contentment. No fault is greater than craving gain. Therefore the contentment that knows contentment is lasting contentment.

## Chapter 47

Without going out the door, one can know the world. Without looking through the window, one can see the way of heaven. The farther one goes, the less one knows. Therefore the sage knows without traveling, understands without seeing, and accomplishes without forcing.

## Chapter 48

In pursuing learning, one increases day by day. In pursuing the Dao, one decreases day by day. Decrease and decrease again, until one reaches non-forcing. Through non-forcing, nothing is left undone. To take the world, one must often be free of meddling. If one is always meddling, one is not fit to take the world.

## Chapter 49

The sage has no fixed private mind; he takes the people's minds as his own. Those who are good, I treat as good. Those who are not good, I also treat as good; by this, goodness is cultivated. Those who are trustworthy, I trust. Those who are not trustworthy, I also trust; by this, trust is cultivated. The sage lives in the world with a gathered heart. The people all turn their eyes and ears toward him, and he treats them like children.

## Chapter 50

People come into life and move toward death. Three in ten are followers of life; three in ten are followers of death; and three in ten who could live move toward death because they overvalue life. Why? Because they are too attached to preserving life. It is said that one skilled in preserving life travels on land without meeting rhinoceros or tiger, enters battle without being harmed by weapons. The rhinoceros finds no place to use its horn; the tiger finds no place to use its claws; the weapon finds no place to use its blade. Why? Because he has no place of death.

## Chapter 51

The Dao gives life to all things. Virtue nourishes them. Matter shapes them. Circumstance completes them. Therefore all things honor the Dao and value virtue. The Dao is honored and virtue valued not because anyone commands it, but because it is naturally so. Thus the Dao gives life, and virtue nourishes: they grow things, raise things, settle things, mature things, shelter things, and protect things. To give life without possessing, to act without relying on the act, to lead without dominating: this is mysterious virtue.

## Chapter 52

The world has a beginning, which may be called the mother of the world. Knowing the mother, one knows the children. Knowing the children, one returns to guard the mother, and throughout life there is no danger. Block the openings, close the gates, and life will not be worn out. Open the openings, multiply affairs, and life cannot be saved. Seeing the small is clarity. Guarding softness is strength. Use the light, but return to its source. Do not bring disaster upon yourself. This is called practicing constancy.

## Chapter 53

If I had even a little knowledge, I would walk on the great road and fear only straying from it. The great road is broad and level, yet people love side paths. The court is splendid while the fields are full of weeds and the granaries are empty. Some wear decorated clothing, carry sharp swords, feast beyond satisfaction, and possess more wealth than they need. This is the boasting of robbers. It is not the Dao.

## Chapter 54

What is well planted cannot be uprooted. What is well held cannot slip away. Descendants will continue the ancestral offerings without interruption. Cultivate the Dao in yourself, and virtue becomes real. Cultivate it in the family, and virtue becomes abundant. Cultivate it in the village, and virtue becomes lasting. Cultivate it in the state, and virtue becomes rich. Cultivate it in the world, and virtue becomes universal. Therefore observe the self through the self, the family through the family, the village through the village, the state through the state, and the world through the world. How do I know the world is like this? By this.

## Chapter 55

One who is rich in virtue is like a newborn child. Poisonous insects do not sting him, wild beasts do not seize him, birds of prey do not strike him. His bones are weak and his tendons soft, yet his grip is firm. He does not yet know the union of male and female, yet his life force is complete. He cries all day without becoming hoarse; this is perfect harmony. Knowing harmony is constancy. Knowing constancy is clarity. Forcing life to grow is inauspicious. Letting the mind drive the breath too hard is aggression. Things that become too strong soon grow old. This is not the Dao. What is not the Dao soon ends.

## Chapter 56

Those who know do not speak much. Those who speak much do not know. Block the openings, close the gates, blunt the sharpness, untangle the knots, soften the brightness, and join with the dust. This is called mysterious sameness. Therefore it cannot be gained by closeness or lost by distance; it cannot be gained by benefit or lost by harm; it cannot be gained by honor or lost by disgrace. For this reason it is honored by the world.

## Chapter 57

Govern the state with uprightness. Use surprise in warfare. Take the world by not meddling. How do I know this is so? By this: the more prohibitions there are, the poorer the people become. The more sharp tools people possess, the more disordered the state becomes. The more clever skills people have, the more strange things arise. The more laws are displayed, the more thieves appear. Therefore the sage says: I do not force, and the people transform themselves. I love stillness, and the people correct themselves. I do not meddle, and the people become rich. I have no desire, and the people become simple.

## Chapter 58

When government is relaxed and generous, the people are simple. When government is sharp and intrusive, the people become cunning. Misfortune is where fortune rests; fortune is where misfortune hides. Who knows where it will end? There is no fixed correctness. The correct may become strange; the good may become harmful. People have been confused for a long time. Therefore the sage is square but does not cut, sharp but does not wound, straight but not reckless, bright but not dazzling.

## Chapter 59

In governing people and serving heaven, nothing is better than restraint. Only restraint can be called early return. Early return means accumulating virtue. Accumulating virtue means nothing cannot be overcome. When nothing cannot be overcome, no one knows the limit. One whose limit cannot be known can possess the state. Possessing the mother of the state, one can last long. This is called deep roots and a firm base, the way of long life and enduring vision.

## Chapter 60

Governing a great state is like cooking a small fish. If the world is governed with the Dao, spirits will not harm people. It is not that spirits lack power, but that their power does not injure people. Not only do spirits not injure people; the sage also does not injure people. Since neither injures the other, their virtues return together to the people.

## Chapter 61

A great state should be like the lower stream, the place where all under heaven gathers, the feminine of the world. The feminine overcomes the masculine through stillness, because stillness takes the lower position. Therefore if a great state lowers itself before a small state, it wins the small state. If a small state lowers itself before a great state, it is accepted by the great state. Some gain by taking the lower position; others gain by being received from below. The great state wants only to gather and nourish people. The small state wants only to enter and serve. If both get what they need, the great state especially should stay low.

## Chapter 62

The Dao is the refuge of all things. It is the treasure of good people and the protection of those who are not good. Fine words can buy honor; fine deeds can raise a person above others. But how can those who are not good be abandoned? Therefore at the enthronement of the emperor and the appointment of the three ministers, offering jade disks and four-horse chariots is not as good as sitting still and offering this Dao. Why did the ancients value the Dao? Was it not because those who seek by it obtain, and those with fault can be forgiven? Therefore it is valued by the world.

## Chapter 63

Act through non-forcing. Handle affairs through non-meddling. Taste what has no strong flavor. Treat the small as great and the few as many. Respond to resentment with virtue. Plan for difficulty while it is still easy. Work on greatness while it is still small. The difficult things in the world begin as easy things; the great things in the world begin as small things. Therefore the sage never tries to be great, and so he can accomplish greatness. One who promises too lightly is rarely trusted. One who treats things as too easy will meet many difficulties. Therefore the sage treats things as difficult, and in the end has no difficulty.

## Chapter 64

What is stable is easy to hold. What has not yet appeared is easy to plan for. What is brittle is easy to break. What is tiny is easy to scatter. Deal with things before they arise. Govern disorder before it appears. A tree that fills the arms grows from a tiny sprout. A nine-story tower rises from baskets of earth. A journey of a thousand miles begins under the feet. One who forces things ruins them. One who grasps things loses them. Therefore the sage does not force, and so does not fail; he does not grasp, and so does not lose. People often fail when they are close to success. If one is as careful at the end as at the beginning, there will be no failure. Therefore the sage desires not to desire and does not prize rare goods. He learns not to overlearn and returns to what others pass by. He helps all things be natural and does not dare to force them.

## Chapter 65

Those in ancient times who were good at the Dao did not use cleverness to enlighten the people; they kept the people simple. People are hard to govern when they have too much cleverness. Therefore governing a state through cleverness is a thief of the state. Governing a state without cleverness is a blessing to the state. Knowing these two is also knowing the standard. Constantly knowing this standard is called mysterious virtue. Mysterious virtue is deep and far-reaching. It moves opposite to things and finally reaches great harmony.

## Chapter 66

Rivers and seas can be kings of the hundred valleys because they are good at staying below them. Therefore they can be kings of the valleys. If the sage wants to stand above the people, he must speak from below. If he wants to stand before the people, he must place himself behind them. Thus when the sage is above, the people do not feel burdened; when he is before them, they do not feel harmed. The world gladly supports him and does not tire of him. Because he does not contend, no one in the world can contend with him.

## Chapter 67

Everyone in the world says my Dao is great, yet seems unlike anything. Because it is great, it is unlike anything. If it were like something, it would have long since become small. I have three treasures that I hold and protect. The first is compassion. The second is frugality. The third is not daring to be first in the world. Because of compassion, one can be brave. Because of frugality, one can be broad. Because of not daring to be first, one can become the leader of all things. Today, to abandon compassion and seek bravery, to abandon frugality and seek breadth, to abandon staying behind and rush ahead: this is death. With compassion in battle, one wins; with compassion in defense, one is secure. When heaven wants to save someone, it protects him with compassion.

## Chapter 68

One who is good as a commander is not aggressive. One who is good at fighting is not angry. One who is good at defeating an enemy does not meet him in direct contention. One who is good at using people places himself below them. This is called the virtue of not contending. This is called using people's strength well. This is called matching heaven, the highest pattern of old.

## Chapter 69

There is a saying in military strategy: I do not dare to be the host; I would rather be the guest. I do not dare advance an inch; I would rather retreat a foot. This is called advancing without formation, rising without showing the arm, confronting without facing an enemy, holding without weapons. No disaster is greater than underestimating the enemy. To underestimate the enemy nearly costs me my three treasures. Therefore when opposing armies are equal, the one with grief and compassion wins.

## Chapter 70

My words are very easy to understand and very easy to practice, yet no one in the world can understand or practice them. Words have an ancestor; actions have a ruler. Because people do not know this, they do not know me. Those who know me are few; therefore I am precious. Thus the sage wears coarse cloth but carries jade within.

## Chapter 71

To know that one does not know is best. Not knowing but thinking one knows is illness. The sage is free of illness because he treats this illness as illness. Because he treats illness as illness, he is not ill.

## Chapter 72

When people no longer fear authority, great authority arrives. Do not crowd their homes. Do not make their lives unbearable. Because you do not make them weary, they will not become weary of you. Therefore the sage knows himself but does not display himself; loves himself but does not exalt himself. He leaves that and takes this.

## Chapter 73

Bravery that dares too much leads to death. Bravery that dares not force things preserves life. Of these two, one may benefit and one may harm. Who knows why heaven dislikes what it dislikes? Even the sage finds this difficult. The way of heaven does not contend, yet it wins well. It does not speak, yet it answers well. It does not summon, yet things come of themselves. It is spacious and slow, yet plans well. Heaven's net is vast; its mesh is wide, yet nothing slips through.

## Chapter 74

If people do not fear death, what use is threatening them with death? If people could always be made to fear death, and someone still acted strangely, I could seize and kill him; who would dare? But there is already one who presides over killing. To kill in place of the one who presides over killing is like chopping wood in place of the master carpenter. Few who chop for the master carpenter avoid injuring their own hands.

## Chapter 75

People are hungry because those above consume too much tax; therefore they are hungry. People are hard to govern because those above interfere too much; therefore they are hard to govern. People take death lightly because the burden of seeking life is too heavy; therefore they take death lightly. Only those who do not overvalue the effort to preserve life are wiser than those who cling to life.

## Chapter 76

When alive, people are soft and weak. When dead, they are hard and stiff. When alive, grasses and trees are soft and tender. When dead, they are dry and brittle. Therefore hardness and strength belong to death; softness and weakness belong to life. Thus a force that is too strong will not win, and a tree that is too hard will be cut down. The strong and great occupy the lower position; the soft and weak occupy the higher position.

## Chapter 77

The way of heaven is like drawing a bow. What is high is pressed down; what is low is lifted up. What has excess is reduced; what lacks is supplemented. The way of heaven reduces excess and supplements lack. The way of human beings is not so: it takes from those who lack and gives to those who already have excess. Who can take what is excessive in himself and offer it to the world? Only one who has the Dao. Therefore the sage acts without relying on the act, completes the work without occupying it, and does not wish to display his worth.

## Chapter 78

Nothing in the world is softer and weaker than water, yet nothing surpasses it in overcoming the hard and strong. Nothing can replace it. That the weak overcome the strong and the soft overcome the hard: everyone in the world knows this, yet no one can practice it. Therefore the sage says: one who receives the dirt of the state is called lord of the altars; one who bears the misfortune of the state is called king of the world. Straight words seem reversed.

## Chapter 79

When a great resentment is reconciled, some resentment is sure to remain. How can this be called good? Therefore the sage holds the left half of the contract and does not demand payment from others. One with virtue keeps to the contract. One without virtue concerns himself with collecting. The way of heaven has no favorites; it is always with the good person.

## Chapter 80

Let the state be small and the people few. Let there be tools of tenfold and hundredfold use, yet not use them. Let people take death seriously and not migrate far away. Though there are boats and carriages, there is nowhere to ride them. Though there are armor and weapons, there is nowhere to display them. Let people return to knotting cords for records. Let them find their food sweet, their clothing beautiful, their homes peaceful, and their customs joyful. Neighboring states may be within sight, and the sounds of roosters and dogs may be heard across the border, yet the people grow old and die without going back and forth.

## Chapter 81

True words are not ornate. Ornate words are not true. Those who are good do not argue. Those who argue are not good. Those who know are not widely showy. Those who are widely showy do not know. The sage does not hoard. The more he does for others, the more he has. The more he gives to others, the more abundant he becomes. The way of heaven benefits and does not harm. The way of the sage acts and does not contend.
